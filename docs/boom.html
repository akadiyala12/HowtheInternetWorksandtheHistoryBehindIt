.Com Bubble

What was it?

The dot-com bubble (also known as the dot-com boom, the tech bubble, and the Internet bubble) was a stock market bubble that occurred in the late 1990s as a result of excessive investment in Internet-related companies during a time of rapid growth in the use and acceptance of the Internet.

The Nasdaq Composite stock market index soared 400 percent between 1995 and its peak in March 2000, only to fall 78 percent from its peak by October 2002, wiping out all of the gains made during the bubble. Many online retailers, such as Pets.com, Webvan, and Boo.com, as well as networking firms, such as Worldcom, NorthPoint Communications, and Global Crossing, collapsed during the crash.

Who were the major players?

eBay is a prominent online auction and shopping site founded by Pierre Omidyar in 1995. Early on, eBay experienced phenomenal growth, with the number of hosted auctions jumping from 250,000 in 1996 to 2 million in the first month of 1997. On September 21, 1998, eBay went public at a $18 IPO price; on the first day of trading, prices soared to $53. eBay extended its product categories to include almost everything that could be sold, from antiques and gold coins to cars and real estate, as well as new, more common auction styles.

Amazon, founded by Jeff Bezos in 1994, is the world's largest online retailer. Amazon began as an online bookstore in 1995, gradually expanding to include movies, music, electronics, computer applications, and a wide range of other consumer products.Amazon went public on May 15, 1997, at a price of $18 per share, growing to more than $100 before plummeting to less than $10 after the dot-com bubble burst. Amazon's business strategy, like that of other dotcoms, prioritized brand awareness over revenue, and the company did not break even until the fourth quarter of 2001.

We all know what these companies have grown to now.
