Invention of the Internet

How did it come to be?

Many scientists predicted the presence of global communication networks even before the infrastructure to actually create the internet existed. In the early 1900s, Nikola Tesla toyed with the concept of a "world wireless machine," and in the 1930s and 1940s, innovative thinkers like Paul Otlet and Vannevar Bush envisioned mechanized, searchable book and media storage systems.

With the development of ARPANET, or the Advanced Research Projects Agency Network, in the late 1960s, the first working prototype of the Internet was born. ARPANET, which was originally supported by the United States Department of Defense, used packet switching to enable multiple computers to communicate over a single network.

ARPANET

ARPAnet sent its first message on October 29, 1969: a “node-to-node” correspondence from one device to another. (The first computer, which was the size of a small building, was housed in a UCLA research lab, and the second was housed in a Stanford research lab.) Despite the fact that the message—“LOGIN”—was short and easy, it crashed the fledgling ARPA network: The Stanford computer only got the first two letters.

On January 1, 1983, ARPANET adopted TCP/IP, and researchers started to put together the “network of networks” that would eventually become the modern Internet. When computer scientist Tim Berners-Lee created the World Wide Web in 1990, the online world took on a more familiar shape.Although it's frequently confused with the internet, the web is simply the most popular way of accessing data online via websites and hyperlinks.
